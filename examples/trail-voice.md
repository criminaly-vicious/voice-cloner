# Voice and style of Trail

Trail speaks to people who have already lost money to poor routes and have no patience for vague promises. Every text starts with a verifiable fact, usually a number, and offers a solution only afterward. We do not sell transformation; we sell fewer wasted miles. When we do not know something, we say so, because a fleet manager's trust is earned by naming a limitation before they discover it themselves.

## When to use

Apply this voice to:

- Website and landing-page copy, including headlines, subheadings, product sections, and pricing pages.
- Campaign, nurture, and onboarding emails for prospects and active customers.
- Sales proposals and materials used by anyone on the sales team.
- Product changelogs, release notes, and end-user help documentation.

Do not apply it to code, informal internal team messages, or formal legal communications governed by separate templates.

## Absolute rules

- Open every text with a concrete fact, preferably numeric, before making a promise. The reader must recognize their reality in the first sentence.
- Spell out a number only when it is below ten; use numerals for the rest because fleet managers scan pages for numbers.
- Name the problem using the customer's everyday language, such as "broken route," "late delivery," or "idle driver," instead of internal technical terminology.
- State a product limitation whenever it matters to the buying decision. Say plainly what Trail does not do.
- End with one specific action, never two options. Two options at the end reduce the likelihood that either happens.
- Never use superlatives about the product. Avoid claims such as revolutionary, market-leading, innovative, or unmatched.

## Vocabulary

The vocabulary comes from the warehouse floor, not the marketing office.

Use sector terms naturally: route, delivery window, load utilization, cost per mile, idle time, route planning, cube utilization, and last mile.

Keep established industry terms as used by the audience. Replace fashionable business jargon with plain operational language: "insight" becomes "finding," "customer success" becomes "support team," and "onboarding" becomes "first week" when the plainer term is clearer.

Avoid consultancy-brochure words, specifically synergy, ecosystem, journey, empower, leverage, disruptive, and game-changing.

## Syntax and rhythm

Sentences are short when making a claim and longer when explaining it. The average is around fifteen words, with deliberate variation to hold the attention of someone reading on a phone in a loading yard.

Paragraphs contain no more than three sentences. They rarely begin with a transition and usually enter directly into the fact.

Public writing uses active voice almost everywhere. Passive voice appears only when the agent genuinely does not matter, as in "the report is generated every Monday."

Avoid automatic contrast structures such as "it is not just software; it is a partner." The target audience distrusts this kind of sentence.

## Tone by register

Website and landing-page copy use the most edited register: shorter sentences, immediate clarity, and no internal jargon because the reader has little patience.

Email and sales proposals use the middle register, with more space to explain tradeoffs, admit limitations, and cite sources for numbers.

Changelogs and documentation use the driest register: verbs early in the sentence, high technical precision, and no decoration because the reader already has a problem open on screen.

Every register keeps the same order—fact before promise—rejects superlatives, and names relevant product limitations.

## Speech mode and writing mode

For spoken text such as video scripts, sales presentations, or support recordings, allow natural contractions and direct audience address. Sentences may begin with "look" or "here is the point." Round numbers may replace precise ones when precision would sound robotic aloud.

For written text, default to revised prose without conversational filler. Use exact numbers and cite a source when a number comes from outside the company.

When the request does not specify a mode, use writing mode.

## Calibrated examples

### Example 1: website headline

Incorrect, generic AI voice:

> Revolutionize your logistics with the complete intelligent route-planning solution that transforms your fleet's journey.

Correct, Trail's voice:

> Your fleet drives 22 percent farther than it needs to. Trail calculates the excess route and gives those miles back to you.

### Example 2: cold prospect email

Incorrect, generic AI voice:

> Hello! I hope you are doing well. I would like to introduce our innovative platform, which can empower your logistics operation. Can we schedule a conversation?

Correct, Trail's voice:

> A carrier your size typically loses 15 to 20 percent of its fuel budget to poorly closed routes. Trail measures that loss in one week without replacing your current system. Reply with the number of vehicles you run today, and we will calculate the number for your operation.

### Example 3: admitting a limitation in a proposal

Incorrect, generic AI voice:

> Our comprehensive platform meets every operational need with complete flexibility for any scenario.

Correct, Trail's voice:

> Trail does not manage vehicle maintenance or driver payroll, and we will not pretend it does. It handles route planning and load utilization. If maintenance is your main problem today, the gain here will be small, and you should know that before signing.

### Example 4: changelog

Incorrect, generic AI voice:

> We are thrilled to announce incredible improvements to the route-planning experience, making everything smoother and more intuitive.

Correct, Trail's voice:

> Routes with more than 200 stops now finish in under 40 seconds, down from 3 minutes. Nothing changes on your screen; the calculation simply returns faster.

### Example 5: help documentation

Incorrect, generic AI voice:

> Easily optimize your route by accessing our intuitive dashboard and leveraging the powerful configuration options available to you.

Correct, Trail's voice:

> Open **Routes**, select the delivery date, and choose **Recalculate**. A route with up to 200 stops returns in under 40 seconds. Trail does not save the previous sequence after recalculation, so export it first if you need a copy.

## Self-validation checklist

- The text opens with a concrete fact rather than a promise.
- No sentence uses a superlative about the product.
- No prohibited jargon appears, including synergy, journey, disruptive, or game-changing.
- Numbers greater than nine use numerals.
- No paragraph contains more than three sentences.
- The ending gives one specific action rather than two alternatives.
- Any product limitation relevant to the reader is stated plainly.

## Known limitations

The source analysis covered website, email, and changelog material but almost no social media. Rules for short social posts are hypotheses rather than evidence. Produce two versions and validate them with the team before publishing.

There is not enough evidence for crisis communication, such as outage notices or serious-error updates. Build that register from real material when the first need arises.
