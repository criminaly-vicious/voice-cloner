# A brand is not a person

The base protocol was designed for idiolect, the voice of an individual. A brand has four structural differences that change the work. Ignoring them produces a brand voice that sounds like a random member of the marketing team.

## Difference 1: a brand has many authors

An individual carries one voice without effort. A brand has many writers, so collected material is usually a mixture of individual voices under an institutional layer.

- Look for the intersection rather than the average: what remains constant across different authors. Averaging distinct voices produces the generic text this process is meant to prevent.
- When two voices conflict—for example, a formal website and slang-heavy social media—do not force them together. Name both, show the inconsistency to the client, and ask which one represents the intended voice.

## Difference 2: a brand has a declared tone and a practiced tone

Many brands have a document calling them "approachable, innovative, and human" while their published writing is bureaucratic and cold.

- Compare the declared guide with published samples and report the distance between them. That gap is often the most valuable finding in the analysis.
- Decide with the client which tone governs generation. If the brand wants to migrate from practiced tone to declared tone, the skill becomes prescriptive rather than descriptive, and the final document must say so.

## Difference 3: a brand needs stricter rules than a person

An individual has intuition for self-correction. A brand voice is applied by many people and several AIs, so it must close more interpretive gaps.

- Include required terminology with exact product and concept names, spelling, and terms that must never be used as synonyms.
- Include compliance boundaries covering statements prohibited for legal, regulatory, or positioning reasons. For a brand, a wrong sentence is a risk, not merely an aesthetic flaw.
- Include a channel matrix because the same core voice behaves differently on websites, in email, in sales proposals, on social media, and in technical documentation.

## Difference 4: a brand has an audience; a person has an interlocutor

- Describe the audience's sophistication, familiarity with technical vocabulary, and dislikes. Tone only makes sense in relation to a reader.
- Record the desired relationship: whether the brand treats the reader as a peer, student, customer, or guest. This choice determines pronouns, imperatives, and the amount of explanation.

## What remains the same

The core process does not change. All three phases remain mandatory, cited evidence remains the standard of truth, calibrated examples remain more valuable than abstract rules, and the same blind acceptance test applies. For a brand, the brand owner judges the result, ideally with more than one team member.
