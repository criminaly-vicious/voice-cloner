# Voice extraction protocol

This document defines what to analyze in the samples. It expands Phase 2. The rule is simple: every claim in the report must cite a real excerpt as evidence. Without a citation, it is a guess.

## Dimension 1: Vocabulary

Vocabulary is the most visible layer of voice and the easiest to exaggerate.

- Collect words and expressions repeated across different samples. Repetition across contexts is a signature; repetition within one sample may only reflect its topic.
- Separate topic vocabulary—words required by the subject—from voice vocabulary the person would use on any subject. Confusing them produces a skill that can only write about topics found in the samples.
- Record foreign terms kept in their original language and terms the person translates. This boundary is highly individual and revealing.
- List words the person evidently avoids when systematic absence can be observed in contexts where those words would be expected.

## Dimension 2: Syntax

- Measure average sentence length and variation. Long linked clauses sound completely different from short sentences even when they use the same vocabulary.
- Identify sentence-opening patterns. Some people begin with a reference such as "this means," others with a direct subject, and others with a question.
- Observe preferences for active or passive voice, coordination or subordination, and direct assertion or hedging.
- Map characteristic punctuation, especially em dashes, semicolons, colons, parentheses, and ellipses. Punctuation is often an unconscious fingerprint.

## Dimension 3: Rhythm and structure

- Describe how the person builds an argument from beginning to end: context first, thesis first, anecdote first, or directly in the middle of the issue.
- Record how they close. Generic AI voice is especially visible in endings. A question, next action, summary, or simply stopping when the subject is complete are different signatures.
- Note paragraph density, list usage, and the balance between prose and visual structure.

## Dimension 4: Personal markers

- Collect speech crutches and determine whether they survive in writing. The difference defines the two modes.
- Find recurring metaphors and semantic fields. The source of a person's imagery—sports, war, engineering, nature, or music—often transfers to new subjects.
- Identify humor, when present, and its type. Poorly calibrated humor is the fastest route from imitation to parody.

## Dimension 5: Tone by context

No person has only one voice. They have an invariant core and variations by register.

- Separate samples by audience and purpose, then describe changes in formality, technical density, slang, and emotional distance.
- Name two to four clear registers, such as institutional, internal, casual, and public, and describe each in operational terms.
- State what does not change between registers. This invariant core is what the AI must always preserve.

## Dimension 6: Speech versus writing

Treat them as separate systems.

- Speech carries hesitation, repetition, self-correction, and unfinished sentences. These are not noise when the final text must sound spoken, such as a script, voice message, or presentation.
- Revised writing shows how the person sounds after having time to decide. Use it as the default unless the request explicitly calls for spoken text.
- Traits found in both modes are invariants and receive the highest priority in the skill.

## Voice report format

The Phase 2 report must contain, in this order:

1. A three- or four-sentence voice summary already written in the analyzed voice as the first proof of concept.
2. Findings for all six dimensions, each supported by cited excerpts.
3. An explicit list of what could not be determined, without filling gaps with guesses.
4. Three to five calibration questions using concrete pairs such as "In this situation, would you write X or Y?" built from the gaps listed above.
